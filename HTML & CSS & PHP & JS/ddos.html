Ataki ddos i jak się chronić

What is DDoS and how does it work? Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attacks are some of the most common threats in today's cyber security world. Their goal is to prevent access to Internet services or resources by overloading the server's or network's infrastructure, resulting in their temporary immobilization. Such actions can cause huge financial losses, loss of customer confidence and damage to the image of companies and institutions. Mechanism of DDoS operation A DDoS attack involves a number of infected devices, such as computers, smartphones and even IoT (Internet of Things) devices that have been previously taken over by cybercriminals. These devices form a so-called botnet - a network of infected machines that are controlled by the hackers. When an attack occurs, all the devices in the botnet simultaneously send a huge number of requests to the target server. This artificial traffic quickly exceeds the handling capacity of the infrastructure, leading to a slowdown or complete shutdown of the service under attack. DDoS can be compared to a situation where a huge number of people suddenly enter a small bridge. The bridge, unsuitable for handling such a large number of people, collapses, blocking passage for both the crowd and individuals. Types of DDoS attacks DDoS attacks can be divided into several main categories, which differ in their mode of operation and objectives: 1. volumetric attacks These attacks focus on overloading network bandwidth. Hackers flood the target server with huge amounts of data, often many times exceeding its capacity to handle the traffic. An example of such an attack is: UDP Flood: Attackers send massive packets of data to various ports on the server, forcing it to continuously process information. Amplification Attack: Exploits vulnerabilities of servers such as DNS or NTP to significantly increase the amount of data sent to the victim. 2 Protocol-Level Attacks In this type of attack, hackers exploit weaknesses in communication mechanisms and Internet protocols to drain server resources or network devices. Examples: SYN Flood: The attacker sends a huge number of connection requests (SYN), but does not complete the connection establishment process, leaving the server in a “waiting” state. Ping of Death: Sending incorrectly formatted or oversized ICMP packets, resulting in network-level errors. 3 Application Level Attacks These attacks aim to overload specific applications or services, such as websites, databases or APIs. They operate at a higher level of the OSI stack and are more difficult to detect. HTTP Flood: Attackers generate mass HTTP requests that mimic normal user traffic, causing server overload. Slowloris: Involves sending fragmented HTTP requests, preventing the server from closing connections. 4. Hybrid attacks Some DDoS attacks combine features of several of the above types.These types are particularly dangerous because they make them difficult to detect and neutralize.
